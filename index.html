Introduction

Within the framework of the "Computational Geometry" course of the Computer Science master's degree of the ULB, we have done researches about the Art Gallery Problem. During those researches, we covered problem and found several results about this topic. From defining the problem, its complexity class to implementing algorithms as well as explaining recent results on the usage of irrational numbers, this webpage is a complete summary of all of our research work.

The Art Gallery Problem

First, let us define what is the Art Gallery Problem, its real-life applications and why is it a such interesting problem.

Defining the problem

Real-life applications

This problem has many applications. Starting with the problem itself, that is, to place guards or security cameras to cover a place, such as a museum, a bank or a prison, etc. But where its use is most widespread, is in video games. In particular in the programming of artificial intelligence, 3D rendering, lighting effects. And from this comes its use in robotics, special effects and simulation tools.

Art Gallery Problem is Existential-R complete

In this section, we will cover the \(\exists \mathbb{R}\) complexity class and explain how is the Art Gallery Problem linked to that complexity class.

Existential Theory of Reals (ETR)

The Existential Theory of Reals problem, often called ETR, is a computational problem where the goal is to determine if a formula \(\phi\), containing variables \(x_1, ..., x_n\) where \(x_i \in \mathbb{R}\ \forall\ i \in \{1, ..., n\}\) can be satisfied with a valuation of variables. The formula \(\phi\) is not only composed of real variables, but is also composed of logic operations and other mathematical symbols. In short, the alphabet of \(\phi\) is the following :

\(\{x_1, x_2, ..., x_n, \forall, \exists, \wedge, \vee, \neg, 0, 1, +, −, \cdot, (, ), =, <, \leq\}\).

In other words, does there exist a valuation for the variables \(x_1, ..., x_n\) such that \(\phi\) is satisfied :

\(\exists(x_1, ..., x_n) : \phi(x_1, ..., x_n)\)

\(\exists\mathbb{R}\) class

Knowing the ETR problem, the \(\exists\mathbb{R}\) class is straightforward. Indeed, the \(\exists\mathbb{R}\) is simply the class of problems that can be reduced to ETR in polynomial time.

As proved by Mikkel Abrahamsen, Anna Adamaszek and Tillmann Miltzow, we know that the Art Gallery Problem belongs to \(\exists\mathbb{R}\) . The proof being long and non-trivial, it is not provided here.

The Art Gallery Problem belongs to the \(\exists\mathbb{R}\) class.

The importance of the \(\exists\mathbb{R}\) resides in the fact that we have the following relation where we do not know if the containments are strict yet :

\(\mathcal{NP} \subseteq \exists\mathbb{R} \subseteq PSPACE\)

Art Gallery is Existential-\(\mathbb{R}\) complete

\(\exists\mathbb{R}\)-completeness for a given problem, say \(A\), means that for every other problem in the Existential Theory of Reals, there is a reduction to \(A\), in polynomial time.

The Art Gallery Problem is at least as hard as deciding whether a system of equations and inequalities over \(\mathbb{R}\) has a satisfiable valuation . The latter is called ETR-INV :

Problem ETR-INV is \(\exists\mathbb{R}\)-complete .

After that, Mikkel Abrahamsen, Anna Adamaszek and Tillmann Miltzow have proven the following with a very long and non-trivial proof :

Let \(\phi\) be an instance of ETR-INV. The polygon \(\mathcal{P}(\phi)\) has corners at rational coordinates, which can be computed in polynomial time. Also, there exist constants \(d_1, ..., d_n \in \mathbb{Q}\) such that for any \(x := (x_1, ..., x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n\), \(x\) is a solution to \(\phi\) if and only if there exists a guard set \(G\) of cardinality \(g(\phi)\) containing guards at all the positions \((x_1 + d_1, 0), ..., (x_n + d_n, 0)\) .

In other words, there exists a reduction from ETR-INV to the Art Gallery Problem. Then we can establish the following :

The Art Gallery Problem is \(\exists\mathbb{R}\)-complete .

Firstly, we know, from Theorem 1 , that the Art Gallery Problem belongs to \(\exists\mathbb{R}\). Furthermore, from Theorem 2, we know that ETR-INV problem is \(\exists\mathbb{R}\)-complete. In other words, every other problem of \(\exists\mathbb{R}\) can be reduced to ETR-INV.
From Theorem 3, we know there exists a reduction from ETR-INV to the Art Gallery Problem. Since ETR-INV is \(\exists\mathbb{R}\)-complete and that it can be transformed into an instance of the Art Gallery Problem, then the Art Gallery Problem is also \(\exists\mathbb{R}\)-complete.

Well-known results

Chvátal's watchman theorem and Steve Fisk's short proof

An early result found by Chvátal results in the following theorem which allows us to obtain an upper bound for the number of guards needed. The proof is very short and was introduced by Steve Fisk :

Let \(P\) be a polygon with \(n\) vertices. Then, we can find a guard set \(G \subseteq P\) of cardinality at most \(\lfloor\frac{n}{3}\rfloor\) .

Let \(P\) be a polygon with \(n\) vertices. First, triangulate \(P\) without adding any new vertex. Every triangulation has a 3-colouring with colors \(a, b\) and \(c\). Now, we can define three subsets \(T_a, T_b, T_c\) of vertices of \(P\) where \(T_k\) is the set of vertices coloured with the colour \(k\). It is clear that at least one of the three subsets \(T_a, T_b, T_c\) has a cardinality \(\leq \lfloor\frac{n}{3}\rfloor\). Let us call that set \(T\). We know every point \(q\) of the polygon \(P\) is in one of the triangles and that every triangle has a point of $p$ of \(T\) on it. Since triangles are convex, we can deduce that \(pq \subset P\).

The orthogonal art gallery problem

Kahn, Klawe, and Kleitman have established that any orthogonal polygon is convexly quadrilateralizable . The proof is quite long but is logical and is also explained in Rourke's book . Following this, it is possible to prove that \(\lfloor\frac{n}{4}\rfloor\) guards are sufficient to cover an orthogonal polygon, like Fisk's proof.

Let \(P\) be a orthogonal polygon with \(n\) vertices. Then, we can find a guard set \(S \subset P\) of cardinality at most \(\lfloor\frac{n}{4}\rfloor\) .

Let \(P\) be an orthogonal polygon and \(G\) the quadrilateralization of \(P\). By adding the diagonals to each square, we end up with a planar graph \(G\). Since every planar graph is 4-colorable and the polygon \(P\) is exclusively composed of convex squares. Each guard completely covers at least one square of the quadrilateralization. As in Chvátal's Proof, we can define four subsets and deduce that one of the four subsets has a cardinality \(\lfloor\frac{n}{4}\rfloor\).

Visibility Polygon

For a polygon \(P\), a point \(a\) is visible from a point \(b\), if the line segment \(ab\) is entirely inside \(P\). In other words, let imagine a chain of vertices \(Q = (q_1, q_2,..., q_n)\) in the same plane of \(a\) and \(b\), which can be assimilated to the polygon \(P\). \(a\) and \(b\) are visible to each other if the line segment \(ab\) doesn't intersect any line in the chain \(Q\).

Definition of visibility polygon

The visibility polygon from a point \(a\) can be defined as following : Assuming the point \(a\) is inside the polygon \(P\), the visibility polygon is noted as \(VP(P,a)\), which correspond to the set of points in \(P\) visible by \(a\) .

\(VP(P,a) = \{b|b \in P \space and \space ab \space \bigcap \space P = ab\}\).

Implementation of visibility polygon

To compute the vibility polygon for a specific point \(a\), multiple algorithms with different techniques exist. The most common method is by using the circular sweeping method by sending rays from the point \(a\) to all the verticies \(V_i\) of the polygon \(P\). Then removing the rays which cross any edge of the polygon before reaching the corresponding vertex \(V_i\). And for the rays which can continue in the polygon after reaching the vertex \(V_i\), compute the next intersection with the polygon. At the end all the end point of the rays will form the boundaries of \(V(P,a)\).

The algorithm implementend in the illustration 3 below, is based on the algorithm explained by Asano Tetsuo . He explained how to compute the visibility polygon for a polygon with and without holes, and the complexity at the worst case is \(O(n \space log \space n)\).

Using irrational coordinates for guards

In this section, we will cover the usage of irrational numbers to describe the coordinates of guards. What if irrational numbers can be or cannot be used ?

Monotone polygon

Let us recall the definition of monotone polygon. A polygon P is monotone, with respect to a straight line L, if every line that is perpendicular to L crosses at most 2 times the edges of P.

Rational & irrational lines

Firstly, a rational point is a point whose coordinates are all rational numbers. An irrational point is a point which is not rational. A rational line is a line which contains two rational points. Again, an irrational line is a line which is not rational .

Irrational coordinates for guards are sometimes needed

In this section, we will show, with an example, that there exists a polygon P having integer coordinates for its vertices that can be guarded with 3 guards having irrational coordinates while it is impossible to guard P with less than 4 guards having rational coordinates. To do so, we will follow a construction of a such a polygon given by Mikkel Abrahamsen, Anna Adamaszek, and Tillmann Miltzow .

Firstly, we start with a basic rectangle of dimensions 4x20. Then, we will add a set \(T\) of six triangular "pockets" that we will call accordingly to their position in the polygon :

  • \(T_{lt}\) (left-top)
  • \(T_{lb}\) (left-bottom)
  • \(T_{mt}\) (middle-top)
  • \(T_{mb}\) (middle-bottom)
  • \(T_{rt}\) (right-top)
  • \(T_{rb}\) (right-bottom)

Then, we will also add a set \(Q\) of 4 quadrilateral pockets that will have pretty much the same naming :

  • \(Q_{lt}\) (left-top)
  • \(Q_{lb}\) (left-bottom)
  • \(Q_{rt}\) (right-top)
  • \(Q_{rb}\) (right-bottom)

And finally, we add a set \(R\) of 3 small rectangular pockets (again, named using the same convention) :

  • \(R_{l}\) (left)
  • \(R_{m}\) (middle)
  • \(R_{r}\) (right)

It is clear that for the triangles \(T_{lt}\) and \(T_{lb}\) as well as for the triangles \(T_{rt}\) and \(T_{rb}\), we need a guard on a line connecting them to be sure that both they can be all seen by that guard. We will call those lines \(l_l\) and \(l_r\). Since an image is worth a thousand words, here are illustrations to understand how are drawn those lines \(l_l\) and \(l_r\) :

After that, we need to ensure that the two triangles \(T_{mb}\) and \(T_{mt}\) are also completely seen. Again, we clearly understand that they can be seen by a guard only if that guard lays on the line \(l_m\) connecting them. At the end of the day, we get the polygon \(P\) with the lines \(l_l\), \(l_m\) and \(l_r\) as on the following figure :

Now that we have taken in consideration the triangles of \(T\), we will concentrate our efforts on seeing the whole base rectangle as well as the 4 quadrilaterals of \(Q\). Let us start by studying the image on the left of the figure 9. If we fix the position of the guard on the line \(l\) in such a way that the guard (let us call it \(g_1\)) sees at least a portion of the bottom edge \(e_b\) and a portion of the edge \(e_t\), we can define \(p_b\) as the leftmost point on \(e_b\) that \(g_1\) can see. In a similar way, we define \(p_t\) as the leftmost point seen by \(g_1\) on the top edge \(e_t\). Clearly, if we move \(g_1\) upwards, \(p_b\) will move to the left while \(p_t\) will move to the right. The opposite happens when we move \(g_1\) downwards. Now, let us call \(b\) the bottom-right point of the quadrilateral \(Q_{lt}\) and \(d\) the top-right point of the quadrilateral \(Q_{rt}\). Then, we can create a line containing \(p_t\) and \(b\) and a second line containing \(p_b\) and \(d\). Those two lines will obviously intersect each other and we will call the intersection \(i\). It is clear that the triangle \(ibd\) is the triangle where we need to put a second guard \(g_2\) in order to see everything that remains that \(g_1\) can not see. If we repeat this for every possible position of \(g_1\) on the line \(l\), we obtain a curve that we will call \(C\). The guard \(g_2\) must be placed on the right of the line \(bd\) and on the left of the curve \(C\).

Now, we can apply the same to the right part of the whole polygon \(P\) defined earlier. We get two curves \(c_l\) and \(c_r\) that defines the region where we could guard the whole polygon with 3 guards.

A guard \(g_l\) must be on the line \(l_l\) to guard the triangular pockets \(T_{lt}\) and \(T_{lb}\) but that same guard must be placed at a correct Y-coordinate so that it can also guard the rectangle \(R_l\) that belongs to the polygon \(P\). We work on a similar way to obtain a guard \(g_r\) on the line \(l_r\). And finally, we want to have the last guard \(g_m\) on the line \(l_m\) to guard the two triangles \(T_{lb}\) and \(T_{rt}\), but also in the intersection formed by the two curves \(C_l\) and \(C_r\). Last but not least, we must also place \(g_m\) in such a way that it also sees the rectangle \(R_m\) :

With a computation that will not be detailed here , we can determine that these 3 points exists but have irrational coordinates.

Also, it is clear that if we scale the polygon \(P\) by the LCM of the denominators of the coordinates, we obtain a polygon having vertices with integer coordinates without changing the number of guards needed for the polygon. In conclusion, we have a polygon with vertices having integer coordinates that can be guarded by 3 irrational guards.

It is easy to see that if we move slightly \(g_l\) and \(g_r\) on their line so that they have no more irrational coordinates, we can add a guard \(g_{m1}\) slightly to the left of \(g_m\) and a guard \(g_{m2}\) slightly to the right to correct the small portion of the polygon \(P\) that we do not see after moving \(g_l\) and \(g_r\). We can, of course, remove \(g_m\).

We can see that the polygon \(P\) can be guarded by 3 irrational guards but can not be guarded by less than 4 guards if we can not use irrational coordinates for guards.

Glossary

  • Polygon :A polygon is a plane geometric figure where the consecutive segments forming a cycle are the edges. And where the ends of each edge are the vertices of the polygon.
  • Simple polygon :A simple polygon is a polygon in which the segments forming the cycle do not intersect. That means, two consecutive segments are touching each other only by their vertices and the non-consecutive segments are not touching each other.
  • Orthogonal polygon :An orthogonal polygon is a simple polygon whose edges are aligned with a pair of perpendicular axes. This means that the angles in the polygon are either 90° or 270°.
  • Problem reduction :Reducing a problem A to a problem B is simply the fact of transforming an instance of the problem A in an instance of the problem B.
